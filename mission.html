Accomplished Missions

Department of Space (DOS) is Government of India department mandated with the execution of Indian Space Programme. The Department of Space has evolved the following programmes with the objective of promoting & developing application of space science and space technology:

Department of space, through its agency ISRO, accomplishes space missions to fulfil its vision, mission and objectives. Here are the missions:


Gaganyaan TV-D1 Mission

The Gaganyaan program is the Indian Space Research Organisation's (ISRO) ambitious mission to demonstrate indigenous human spaceflight capability by sending a crew of three astronauts into a low-Earth orbit (LEO) of 400 km for a three-day mission and safely returning them to Earth. This historic endeavor, initially announced in 2018, aims to establish India as the fourth country, after the United States, Russia, and China, to achieve independent human spaceflight.
To ensure the safety and reliability of the crewed mission, ISRO is executing a series of uncrewed test flights before the final human launch. A critical test vehicle (TV-D1) flight was successfully conducted in October 2023 to validate the Crew Escape System, and further uncrewed missions carrying the female humanoid robot Vyommitra are planned for 2025 and 2026. In August 2025, a significant milestone was reached with the successful Integrated Air Drop Test (IADT-01), which validated the parachute-based deceleration system for the crew module's safe splashdown. The first crewed mission is now targeted for the first quarter of 2027.
The Gaganyaan spacecraft, known as the Orbital Module, consists of a Crew Module (CM) and a Service Module (SM). The CM is the pressurized, habitable space for the astronauts, designed to withstand the harsh conditions of re-entry. The SM provides essential support systems, including propulsion, power, and thermal control. For launch, a human-rated version of the LVM3 rocket, ISRO's most powerful launch vehicle, is being used. The four astronaut-designates, all Indian Air Force test pilots, are undergoing rigorous training in India, building upon initial training received in Russia.
The Gaganyaan mission holds immense significance for India, with benefits spanning technological, scientific, and strategic domains. It will foster a robust space ecosystem, inspiring a new generation of scientists and engineers while stimulating economic growth and employment. The mission's success will also be a stepping stone toward more ambitious future goals, including establishing an Indian space station by 2035 and a crewed mission to the Moon by 2040. By demonstrating self-reliance in this complex technology, India solidifies its position as a major player in global space exploration.

ADITYA-L1

The Indian Space Research Organisation's (ISRO) Aditya-L1 is India's first dedicated space-based mission to study the Sun, launched on September 2, 2023. After a 126-day journey, the spacecraft was successfully inserted into a halo orbit around the Sun-Earth Lagrange Point 1 (L1) on January 6, 2024. The strategic placement at L1, approximately 1.5 million kilometers from Earth, offers Aditya-L1 an uninterrupted, continuous view of the Sun without being obscured by eclipses or occultations. This unique vantage point is crucial for studying solar activities and space weather in real-time, providing an early warning system for events that could impact technological infrastructure on Earth.
The mission is designed to address key mysteries in solar physics, such as the mechanisms behind the extreme heating of the solar corona, the drivers of solar wind acceleration, and the origins of coronal mass ejections (CMEs) and solar flares. Aditya-L1 carries seven specialized payloads, developed collaboratively by various Indian scientific institutions and ISRO centers, to observe the Sun's atmosphere in multiple wavelengths. Four of these payloads are designed for remote sensing of the Sun, while the remaining three conduct in-situ studies of the particles and fields at the L1 point.
The remote-sensing instruments include the Visible Emission Line Coronagraph (VELC), which studies the solar corona and the dynamics of CMEs. The Solar Ultraviolet Imaging Telescope (SUIT) captures images of the photosphere and chromosphere in the near-ultraviolet range, providing unprecedented insights into solar flares and activity in the lower solar atmosphere. The Solar Low Energy X-ray Spectrometer (SoLEXS) and the High Energy L1 Orbiting X-ray Spectrometer (HEL1OS) are dedicated to monitoring X-ray flares to understand the coronal heating mechanism and the energy dynamics of solar eruptive events.
Complementing the remote-sensing payloads are three in-situ instruments. The Aditya Solar wind Particle Experiment (ASPEX) and the Plasma Analyser Package for Aditya (PAPA) analyze the solar wind's composition, energy distribution, and particle dynamics. Finally, the Magnetometer (MAG) measures the interplanetary magnetic field, providing critical data for understanding space weather drivers.
Since reaching its orbit, Aditya-L1 has been successfully collecting and transmitting scientific data, leading to groundbreaking observations. For example, the SUIT payload captured the first-ever image of a solar flare "kernel" in the near-ultraviolet band, confirming the linkage between flare energy and coronal plasma temperature. The mission's findings are expected to significantly advance global solar physics research and improve space weather forecasting capabilities, protecting essential satellite and communication systems on Earth.

Chandrayaan-3

Chandrayaan-3 mission achieved a historic soft landing on the Moon's south pole on August 23, 2023, making India the first country to do so. The mission successfully demonstrated end-to-end landing and roving capabilities on the lunar surface, a significant step in India's space exploration program, especially after the partial failure of the Chandrayaan-2 lander in 2019. The Chandrayaan-3 spacecraft consisted of three main components: an indigenous lander module named Vikram, a 26 kg rover named Pragyan, and a propulsion module that ferried the lander and rover to lunar orbit.
After its launch on July 14, 2023, aboard an LVM3 M4 rocket, the spacecraft underwent a series of intricate Earth-bound maneuvers to build up speed before a trans-lunar injection. It successfully entered lunar orbit on August 5, followed by several orbit-reduction and de-boosting maneuvers. On August 17, the Vikram lander separated from the propulsion module to begin its final descent. The soft landing was executed flawlessly, with the lander employing advanced technologies, including a laser Doppler velocimeter and robust landing legs, to navigate hazards and make a gentle touchdown at the chosen site. The landing site, near 69°S latitude, was named "Shiv Shakti Point" by Prime Minister Narendra Modi.
Following the landing, the Pragyan rover was deployed from the Vikram lander via a ramp, leaving imprints of the ISRO logo and the Indian emblem on the lunar soil as it moved. The lander and rover were designed to operate for one lunar day (approximately 14 Earth days) before shutting down for the lunar night's extreme temperatures. During their operational period, the scientific payloads on board collected invaluable data through in-situ experiments. Key findings include:
Confirmation of sulfur and other elements like aluminum, calcium, and iron on the lunar south pole's surface using the Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscope (LIBS).
Measurement of the thermal properties of the lunar topsoil by the Chandra's Surface Thermophysical Experiment (ChaSTE), which revealed significant temperature variations between the surface and a few centimeters below.
Recording of lunar seismic activity by the Instrument for Lunar Seismic Activity (ILSA), detecting movements from the rover and a potential natural moonquake.
Detection of low plasma density near the lunar surface by the Radio Anatomy of Moon Bound Hypersensitive ionosphere and Atmosphere (RAMBHA) instrument.
The discovery that the landing site sits within a 160 km wide, ancient, buried impact crater, providing insight into the Moon's geological evolution.
While the lander and rover entered sleep mode after their operational life, the mission's propulsion module continued its journey. It was successfully moved from lunar orbit to a high Earth orbit in late 2023 to carry out additional scientific observations of Earth, validating new maneuver strategies for future missions. The successful execution of Chandrayaan-3 solidified India's position in global space exploration and demonstrated its technological prowess in overcoming complex challenges.

Mars Orbiter Mission

The Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM), also known as Mangalyaan, was India's first interplanetary mission, launched on November 5, 2013. The mission was a landmark achievement, successfully inserting the spacecraft into Martian orbit on its maiden attempt on September 24, 2014. This made India the first Asian nation and the fourth space agency in the world to accomplish this feat. The mission's success was particularly notable for its remarkably low cost of approximately ₹450 crore.
The primary objectives of the mission were twofold: technological and scientific. The technological goals included designing and operating a spacecraft for the complex journey to Mars, performing trajectory maneuvers, ensuring autonomous functions, and managing deep-space communication. The scientific goals focused on studying the Martian surface features, morphology, mineralogy, and atmosphere using five onboard instruments.
Despite being designed for a life of six months, the orbiter operated for over seven years, providing valuable scientific data until communication was lost in April 2022. Key findings included observing atmospheric escape during a global dust storm and studying the Martian exosphere, which revealed that oxygen can exceed carbon dioxide at certain altitudes. The Mars Colour Camera also captured thousands of high-resolution images of the planet's surface and its moons, Phobos and Deimos, producing a Mars Atlas. The mission showcased India's indigenous technological capabilities and elevated the country's prestige in space exploration.
